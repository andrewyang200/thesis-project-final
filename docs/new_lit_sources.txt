# Key References for Shared Frailty Models and IPTW in Survival/Competing-Risks Analysis

## Overview

This report compiles four peer-reviewed articles that directly support a methodological pivot toward (i) shared frailty or mixed-effects survival models for clustered time-to-event data and (ii) inverse probability of treatment weighting (IPTW) and propensity score weighting for causal inference in survival and competing-risks settings. Each reference includes a brief explanation of its relevance to modeling unobserved heterogeneity (e.g., jurisdiction-level legal culture) and to identifying causal effects using survival outcomes.[1][2][3][4]

## Shared Frailty / Mixed-Effects Survival Models

### 1. Rueten-Budde et al., "Investigating hospital heterogeneity with a competing risks frailty model" (Statistics in Medicine, 2019)

**Full citation**  
Rueten-Budde, Anja J., Hein Putter, and Marta Fiocco. "Investigating hospital heterogeneity with a competing risks frailty model." *Statistics in Medicine* 38, no. 2 (2019): 269–288. doi:10.1002/sim.8002.[3][5]

**Methodological relevance**  
Rueten-Budde et al. develop an additive gamma shared-frailty model for competing-risks data in multicenter clinical trials, where each hospital has center-level frailty terms that capture unobserved heterogeneity in baseline risk and correlation between multiple event types. The paper demonstrates that such center-level random effects can be estimated via an expectation–maximization algorithm and interpreted as latent institutional effects, directly analogous to modeling unobserved jurisdictional "legal culture" via circuit- or district-level frailties in a competing-risks model of settlement versus dismissal.[3]

### 2. Frévent et al., "A Shared-Frailty Spatial Scan Statistic Model for Time-to-Event Data" (Biometrical Journal, 2024)

**Full citation**  
Frévent, Camille, Mohamed-Salem Ahmed, Sophie Dabo-Niang, and Michaël Genin. "A Shared-Frailty Spatial Scan Statistic Model for Time-to-Event Data." *Biometrical Journal* 66, no. 5 (2024): e202300200. doi:10.1002/bimj.202300200.[4][6]

**Methodological relevance**  
Frévent et al. propose a Cox model with spatially correlated shared frailties at the level of geographic units, allowing both within-area dependence in survival times and spatial dependence across neighboring areas to be modeled explicitly. By treating each spatial unit as sharing a latent frailty term, the paper provides a clear template for treating judicial circuits or districts as clusters with shared unobserved risk factors, thereby justifying the use of shared-frailty survival models to address unobserved jurisdictional heterogeneity in federal securities class action outcomes.[7][4]

## IPTW / Propensity Score Weighting for Survival and Competing Risks

### 3. Xie and Liu, "Adjusted Kaplan–Meier estimator and log-rank test with inverse probability of treatment weighting for survival data" (Statistics in Medicine, 2005)

**Full citation**  
Xie, Jun, and Chaofeng Liu. "Adjusted Kaplan–Meier estimator and log-rank test with inverse probability of treatment weighting for survival data." *Statistics in Medicine* 24, no. 20 (2005): 3089–3110. doi:10.1002/sim.2174.[8][1]

**Methodological relevance**  
Xie and Liu introduce an adjusted Kaplan–Meier estimator and weighted log-rank test in which each subject is weighted by the inverse probability of receiving their observed treatment, estimated via a propensity score model, to remove confounding in observational survival comparisons. This paper shows that IPTW can consistently estimate treatment-specific survival functions and provides variance formulas, supporting the use of propensity-score-based IPTW to recover causal survival curves (e.g., pre- vs. post-PSLRA regimes) in right-censored time-to-resolution models.[8][1]

### 4. Austin and Fine, "Inverse Probability of Treatment Weighting Using the Propensity Score With Competing Risks in Survival Analysis" (Statistics in Medicine, 2025)

**Full citation**  
Austin, Peter C., and Jason P. Fine. "Inverse Probability of Treatment Weighting Using the Propensity Score With Competing Risks in Survival Analysis." *Statistics in Medicine* 44, no. 5 (2025): e70009. doi:10.1002/sim.70009.[2]

**Methodological relevance**  
Austin and Fine extend IPTW methodology to settings with competing risks by combining propensity-score-based inverse probability of treatment weights with cumulative incidence estimation via weighted Aalen–Johansen, IPTW–IPCW, and augmented IPTW–IPCW estimators. Their simulations and empirical example (statin treatment and cardiovascular death) demonstrate that IPTW can recover unbiased time-specific risk differences and relative risks under competing risks, directly supporting an IPTW-based causal analysis of how the PSLRA affects the cause-specific risk of settlement versus dismissal over time.[9][2]
