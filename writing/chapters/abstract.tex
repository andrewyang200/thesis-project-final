Federal securities class actions are economically important, but their
resolution has rarely been studied with methods that treat settlement and
dismissal as competing risks. This thesis presents, to our knowledge, the
first population-scale competing-risks survival analysis of federal securities
litigation, using 12,968 cases from the Federal Judicial Center's Integrated
Database (1990--2024). We estimate Aalen-Johansen cumulative incidence
functions, cause-specific Cox models, Fine-Gray subdistribution models, and
inverse probability of treatment weighting (IPTW) to examine how the Private
Securities Litigation Reform Act of 1995 (PSLRA), judicial circuit, and case
characteristics shape resolution timing and type.

The strongest result is an increase in early dismissal after the PSLRA. In a
piecewise Cox model, the dismissal association is largest in the first year
after filing (HR~$= 1.789$), weaker in years one to two (HR~$= 1.255$), and
null thereafter, consistent with an early pleading-stage filter. Evidence for
reduced settlement is directionally similar but less stable across
composition-adjusted, circuit-specific, time-trend, and cluster-robust
specifications. Judicial geography is a structural predictor: settlement
hazards vary sharply across circuits relative to the Second Circuit baseline.
Fine-Gray models show that MDL cases can have lower instantaneous settlement
rates yet higher cumulative settlement probability, illustrating how
single-outcome approaches miss important resolution dynamics. Placebo and
time-trend checks indicate that the estimates should be interpreted as
associational or composition-adjusted rather than causal.

These results show that competing-risks methods improve the empirical
characterization of securities litigation and sharpen the most defensible claim
about the PSLRA: it is associated primarily with more early dismissals.
