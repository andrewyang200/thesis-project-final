This chapter presents the empirical results of the
competing-risks survival analysis described in
Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology}, addressing the central
research question: how do the PSLRA, circuit geography,
and case characteristics jointly shape the timing and type
of resolution in federal securities class actions?
We proceed from nonparametric
characterization of duration and outcomes
(Section~\ref{sec:results_overview}), through layered
assessment of the PSLRA's association with case resolution
(Section~\ref{sec:results_pslra}), geographic disparity
across federal circuits
(Section~\ref{sec:results_circuits}), and case-level
structural characteristics
(Section~\ref{sec:results_characteristics}), to model
diagnostics (Section~\ref{sec:results_diagnostics}) and a
summary of findings (Section~\ref{sec:results_summary}).

%% SECTION 5.1 — OVERVIEW

\section{Overview: Duration and Competing Outcomes}
\label{sec:results_overview}

\subsection{Overall Duration}

Securities class actions resolve rapidly in aggregate:
approximately 28\% of cases terminate within the first year,
and roughly 47\% within two years, reflecting the
concentration of dismissals during the motion-to-dismiss
phase. Resolution slows markedly after year four, with fewer
than 7\% of cases remaining pending beyond eight years
(Figure~\ref{fig:km_overall}).

\subsection{Competing-Risks Cumulative Incidence: Overall}

Dismissal is the dominant resolution pathway, reaching
approximately 37\% cumulative incidence by two years and
51\% by five years, while settlement accumulates more
gradually to 29\% at five years and 39\% at eight years
(Figure~\ref{fig:cif_overall}). The two curves do not
converge, reflecting a fundamental asymmetry: dismissal
operates primarily through early pleading-stage motions,
while settlement accumulates more slowly among cases that
survive the initial motion-to-dismiss gauntlet.

A naive Kaplan-Meier estimate of time to settlement that
censors dismissals would produce upwardly biased settlement
probabilities by treating dismissed cases as though they
remained at risk of eventual settlement. The Aalen-Johansen
curves avoid that distortion and establish the baseline
pattern against which the PSLRA analysis is evaluated.

\section{The PSLRA and Litigation Outcomes}
\label{sec:results_pslra}

The PSLRA is robustly associated with higher dismissal
hazard, while the settlement evidence points in the same
direction but proves materially less stable across
specifications. This section builds the evidence in five layers---nonparametric patterns,
associational estimates, time-varying dynamics,
composition-adjusted inference, and robustness
checks---each progressively narrowing the range of plausible
explanations for the observed divergence.

\subsection{Nonparametric Evidence: CIF by PSLRA Regime}

The PSLRA is associated with divergent shifts in both
competing processes simultaneously and in opposite
directions. Post-PSLRA dismissal incidence rises sharply in
the first year and reaches 52.1\% by five years, above the
pre-PSLRA five-year trajectory of 37.8\%; settlement
incidence is simultaneously suppressed, reaching 26.8\% at
five years compared with 51.0\% pre-PSLRA
(Figure~\ref{fig:cif_pslra}).

\begin{figure}[!htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig_cif_pslra.png}
    \vskip 5pt
    \caption[Cumulative Incidence of Outcomes by PSLRA Regime]{Aalen-Johansen CIFs stratified by PSLRA regime. Solid lines = Pre-PSLRA ($n = 1{,}032$); dashed = Post-PSLRA ($n = 11{,}936$). Blue = settlement; orange/red = dismissal. Gray's test---which evaluates whether CIFs differ significantly across groups in the presence of competing risks---rejects equality for both settlement (statistic $= 131.3$, $p < 0.001$) and dismissal (statistic $= 82.1$, $p < 0.001$).}
    \label{fig:cif_pslra}

Table~\ref{tab:cif_horizons} quantifies differences at four
key horizons. The divergence is particularly pronounced in
the first year: one-year dismissal incidence is 25.4\%
post-PSLRA versus 15.4\% pre-PSLRA, consistent with the
PSLRA's heightened pleading requirements concentrating
dismissals at the pleading stage. The settlement gap widens
steadily: the five-year settlement difference is 24.2
percentage points (51.0\% vs.\ 26.8\%), indicating a sustained
divergence in outcome probabilities rather than merely a
timing shift. Notably, under the pre-PSLRA regime
settlement actually \emph{dominates} dismissal at five
years (51.0\% vs.\ 37.8\%), reflecting a litigation
environment in which cases that survived early motions had
a better-than-even chance of settling. This ordering
reverses post-PSLRA, where five-year dismissal probability
(52.1\%) nearly doubles the settlement probability
(26.8\%).

informative than the test statistics alone.

\subsection{Associational Evidence: Baseline Cause-Specific Cox Models}

Post-PSLRA filing is associated with a 44\% lower
instantaneous settlement hazard (hazard ratio
[HR] $= 0.563$, 95\% CI: 0.511--0.621, $p < 0.001$) and a
41\% higher instantaneous dismissal hazard (HR $= 1.409$,
95\% CI: 1.269--1.565, $p < 0.001$;
Table~\ref{tab:cox_baseline}). In a competing-risks setting, a hazard ratio above one indicates a higher instantaneous rate of experiencing that specific outcome (faster time-to-that-outcome); a hazard ratio below one indicates a lower instantaneous rate. Because settlement and dismissal are competing events, a higher cause-specific hazard for one outcome does not guarantee shorter overall case duration---it reflects the instantaneous rate of that particular terminal event, holding the competing pathway fixed. However, as detailed in
Section~\ref{sec:results_robustness}, the settlement
association is highly sensitive to time-trend controls and
loses statistical significance entirely in circuit-specific
sub-samples.

The Grambsch-Therneau test rejects the proportional hazards
assumption for both settlement ($p < 0.001$) and dismissal
($p < 0.001$), indicating that the PSLRA's association with
hazard rates is not constant over case duration. This
motivates the time-varying analysis in
Section~\ref{sec:piecewise} and implies that the constant
hazard ratios above are best interpreted as time-averaged
summaries of a more complex temporal relationship.

\subsection{Time-Varying PSLRA Associations by Outcome}
\label{sec:piecewise}

The PSLRA's association with dismissal hazard declines
monotonically over case duration: a strong first-year
elevation (HR $= 1.789$, 95\% CI: 1.520--2.105,
$p < 0.001$) consistent with the Act's heightened pleading
requirements and automatic discovery stay concentrating
dismissals at the pleading stage (Table~\ref{tab:piecewise}).
In years one to two, the association attenuates to a modest
26\% elevation that is marginally significant
(HR $= 1.255$, 95\% CI: 1.016--1.550, $p = 0.035$).
Beyond two years, the PSLRA association with dismissal is
indistinguishable from null (HR $= 1.063$, 95\% CI:
0.886--1.276, $p = 0.510$).

The settlement side follows a different temporal pattern.
Post-PSLRA settlement hazard is suppressed in all three
intervals: 48\% lower in the first year
(HR $= 0.518$, $p < 0.001$), 61\% lower in years one to two
(HR $= 0.387$, $p < 0.001$), and 33\% lower after two years
(HR $= 0.673$, $p < 0.001$). Unlike dismissal, the
settlement association does not fade to null within the
piecewise model; its strongest suppression appears in the
middle interval and then attenuates. That pattern should be
read alongside the broader fragility of the settlement
finding under circuit-specific decomposition, cluster-robust
inference, and time-trend controls discussed below.

Taken together, the piecewise results show that time
variation is outcome-specific. Dismissal follows a sharp
early-stage spike that decays to null, while settlement
remains below unity in all three periods. Because the
Grambsch-Therneau tests reject proportional hazards for the
PSLRA term in both outcomes, this decomposition addresses a
documented modeling problem rather than adding a purely
exploratory layer.

\subsection{Composition-Adjusted Evidence: IPTW Triangulation}
\label{sec:results_iptw}

The preceding analyses establish that the PSLRA is associated
with large and statistically significant differences in
hazard rates. A natural question is how much of this raw
association remains after adjusting for observable case-mix
shifts, and how much is sensitive to
concurrent changes in observable case composition. If the
post-PSLRA period sees disproportionately more cases filed
in high-dismissal circuits, for instance, part of the raw
PSLRA association could be an artifact of compositional
shifts rather than a stable period difference under a common
case mix.

To probe that sensitivity, we apply the
four-strategy triangulation framework described in
Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology}, using inverse probability of
treatment weighting to re-weight the pre-PSLRA
control sample to match the post-PSLRA case mix on observed
covariates. The IPTW analysis uses 12,866 cases with
complete covariate data; relative to the full 12,968-case
cohort, 16 D.C.\ Circuit cases are omitted because
circuit-adjusted models exclude circuits with fewer than 50
cases, and 86 additional cases have missing origin.
Statutory basis missingness is retained as an explicit
``Missing'' level rather than treated as an exclusion. The
slight difference in
unadjusted HRs relative to Table~\ref{tab:cox_baseline}
reflects this restricted sample.
Table~\ref{tab:iptw_triangulation} presents the results,
progressing from no adjustment to full IPTW-weighted
estimation.

For dismissal, the composition-adjusted MSM HR is 1.519,
modestly amplified from the unadjusted 1.414. After
conditioning on observed shifts in circuit composition, case
origin, and statutory basis, the PSLRA remains associated
with a 52\% higher instantaneous dismissal hazard. The
amplification is only a point-estimate comparison rather
than a formally tested difference, however, so it should be
read as a sensitivity result rather than as evidence that
weighting has recovered a larger underlying effect.

For settlement, the composition-adjusted MSM HR is 0.741,
attenuated from the unadjusted 0.557. The raw 44\%
reduction in settlement hazard shrinks to a 26\% reduction
after composition adjustment. That arithmetic attenuation is
transparent, but it remains a comparison of point estimates
under different adjustment sets, not a formal decomposition
of how much of the raw settlement gap is explained by case
mix shifts across the pre- and post-PSLRA periods. The
residual association is not
causally attributable to the reform; unmeasured temporal
confounders may account for part or all of it.

Table~\ref{tab:iptw_triangulation} reports 95\% confidence

sampling uncertainty under the weighted specification but do
not resolve the overlap and post-treatment-bias concerns
discussed below.

persists in the same direction, consistent with the hazard

specifications, confirming that the finding is not an
artifact of any particular coding, sampling, or time-trend
choice (Figure~\ref{fig:robustness},
Table~\ref{tab:robustness}).

Five observations warrant emphasis. First, Schemes~B and~C
reclassify Code~12 (voluntary dismissal) and Code~5 (consent
judgment) as settlements, producing settlement HRs of
$\approx 0.79$ compared with 0.563 under Scheme~A. The
PSLRA settlement association is sensitive to disposition
coding but remains significant under all schemes.
Dismissal HRs attenuate from 1.409 to 1.183 under intermediate
coding, with $p$ rising from $< 0.001$ to 0.005.

Second, excluding post-2020 cases has virtually no effect on
estimates, indicating that recently filed cases with high
censoring rates do not distort the results.

Third, the circuit-specific sub-models provide the most
important qualification of the settlement finding. Within
the Second Circuit, the PSLRA settlement HR \emph{crosses
unity} (HR $= 1.052$, $p = 0.762$), and within the Ninth
Circuit the association is non-significant
(HR $= 0.957$, $p = 0.622$). The PSLRA dismissal
association, by contrast, is significant and substantial in
both circuits (Second: HR $= 1.941$; Ninth:
HR $= 1.575$). This pattern suggests that part of the pooled
settlement HR reflects between-circuit composition rather
than a stable within-circuit reform association.

Fourth, the spline time-trend control is the most
parametrically flexible identification check: after
flexibly absorbing nonlinear
secular trends in judicial behavior via a natural cubic
spline ($\text{df} = 3$), the PSLRA dismissal HR
\emph{increases} to 3.515 ($p < 0.001$). The corresponding
PSLRA VIF is 5.32, quantifying substantial overlap between
the reform indicator and the spline basis without collapsing
into exact collinearity. This does not prove
that the dismissal association is free of calendar-time
confounding; rather, it shows that the positive dismissal
association persists, and even enlarges, under one flexible
time-trend specification.
This estimate is an outlier in the robustness range: six of
seven specifications produce dismissal HRs between 1.183
and 1.941, and the spline value reflects a methodologically
distinct specification in which the flexible time trend
absorbs secular variation and amplifies the discrete
step-change at the PSLRA cutoff. It should be read as the
upper bound of a wide but directionally consistent range
rather than a representative central estimate.
The settlement HR attenuates to 0.714 and remains significant
($p = 0.013$) under the spline specification, but the
attenuation from 0.563 indicates that part of the raw
settlement suppression is confounded with secular trends.

Fifth, across all seven specifications the dismissal HR
ranges from 1.183 to 3.515 and the settlement HR ranges from
0.558 to 1.052. The dismissal direction is unanimous and
statistically significant in every specification. The
settlement direction is consistent in six of seven
specifications but crosses unity in the Second Circuit
sub-sample and loses significance in both circuit-specific
analyses.

\subsubsection*{Temporal Identification and Placebo Results}

To further probe whether the observed PSLRA association
is robust to pre-existing trends, we
implement the identification strategies described in
Section~\ref{sec:temporal_id}.

\textbf{Clean-window analysis (1993--1998).} Restricting the
sample to a narrow six-year window ($n = 1{,}235$)
surrounding the PSLRA's effective date yields a dismissal
HR of 1.482 (95\% CI: 1.243--1.768, $p < 0.001$) and a
settlement HR of 0.724 (95\% CI: 0.607--0.864, $p < 0.001$).
Both associations remain significant and directionally
consistent with the full-sample baseline.

\textbf{Placebo cutoff test (1992).} As a falsification
check, we construct a placebo ``law'' at January~1, 1992 and
re-estimate on the pre-PSLRA sample only ($n = 1{,}032$,
spanning 1990--1995). The placebo produces a significant
dismissal HR of 0.733 (95\% CI: 0.588--0.913, $p = 0.006$)
and a settlement HR of 1.638 (95\% CI: 1.289--2.082,
$p < 0.001$). The placebo dismissal HR is below unity,
indicating a \emph{downward} pre-existing trend in dismissal
rates during the early 1990s---the opposite direction from
the PSLRA's subsequent upward association. That directional
contrast rules out a simple monotone story in which an
upward pre-trend mechanically inflates the later dismissal
estimate, but it also shows that period-specific dynamics
were already moving before the law changed. The model's
ability to detect such effects in the absence of any
legislative change precludes clean causal attribution. This
reinforces our decision to frame all estimates as
associational or composition-adjusted.

\subsubsection*{Synthesis: The Weight of Evidence}

Across these evidence layers, one conclusion is stable and
one is qualified. The dismissal association is consistently
positive and strongest early in the life of the case. The
settlement association is directionally similar in many
specifications but substantially more fragile, failing to
survive cluster-robust inference or the Second- and
Ninth-Circuit sub-samples.

\section{Geographic Disparity Across Federal Circuits}
\label{sec:results_circuits}

Circuit geography is a dominant predictor of securities litigation outcomes. Settlement hazard ratios relative to the Second Circuit range from 1.1 to 4.3 times across circuits, indicating that judicial venue substantially shapes the likelihood of settlement at any given duration. The PSLRA's association with outcomes is not uniform across jurisdictions: likelihood ratio tests reject a homogeneous PSLRA coefficient in favor of circuit-specific responses, and a shared frailty analysis detects residual unobserved heterogeneity after conditioning on observed covariates.

\subsection{Cumulative Incidence by Circuit}

Circuit heterogeneity in resolution patterns is substantial,
and the patterns are not symmetric across outcomes---a
finding that the competing-risks framework reveals and that
standard single-outcome analyses would miss
(Figures~\ref{fig:cif_circuit_dismissal}
and~\ref{fig:cif_circuit_settlement}).

(settlement SHR $= 0.503$ vs.\ CS HR $= 0.784$; dismissal

occurs because the PSLRA is associated with increased

model holds it fixed.

The MDL comparison is the most analytically revealing
divergence. The intuition is this: MDL cases resolve slowly,
but because they are almost never \emph{dismissed}, the cases
that would have exited via dismissal in a non-MDL setting
instead remain available to settle, and over time more of
them do. A lower rate of settlement at any given moment can
produce a \emph{higher} cumulative probability of settlement
when the competing exit route is nearly shut off.

Despite the substantially lower cause-specific
settlement hazard (CS HR $= 0.490$), the Fine-Gray
subdistribution hazard ratio for settlement is

95\% CI: 2.241--2.717, $p < 0.001$). The reconciliation
lies in the competing pathway: MDL cases have an
extremely low dismissal hazard (CS HR $= 0.106$), so the
pool of MDL cases available to settle remains large
throughout follow-up. Despite resolving more slowly at any
given moment, MDL cases accumulate substantially higher
cumulative settlement probability than non-MDL cases
precisely because so few are dismissed along the way. This
sign reversal---a lower instantaneous settlement rate
producing a \emph{higher} cumulative settlement
probability---is visible only through competing-risks
analysis and represents one of the strongest
methodological arguments for the dual-framework approach
used in this thesis.

\section{Model Diagnostics and Validation}

These violations motivate two responses already
incorporated in our analysis: the piecewise Cox model
(Section~\ref{sec:results_pslra}) decomposes the
time-varying PSLRA association directly, and all constant-HR
estimates carry the time-averaged interpretation established
in Section~\ref{sec:results_pslra}.

\subsection{IPTW Covariate Balance}
\label{subsec:iptw_balance}

The validity of the composition-adjusted estimates in
Section~\ref{sec:results_pslra} depends on the IPTW
weights achieving adequate balance between the pre-PSLRA
($n = 1{,}031$) and post-PSLRA ($n = 11{,}835$) cohorts on
all observed covariates. We use ATT (average treatment
effect on the treated) weights with 99th-percentile
trimming (cap $\approx 43.5$), which reweight the smaller
pre-PSLRA cohort to match the post-PSLRA covariate
distribution.

Figure~\ref{fig:iptw_balance} presents the Love plot---a
diagnostic display in which each row is a covariate or the
propensity-score distance summary and the horizontal axis
shows the standardized mean difference (SMD) between treated
and control groups before and after
weighting; covariates falling within $|\text{SMD}| < 0.1$
are considered adequately balanced. After weighting, all 19
covariate rows achieve $|\text{SMD}| < 0.10$, the
conventional threshold for adequate balance
\citep{austinfine2025}; the adjusted propensity-score
distance is 0.053, and the largest covariate residual
imbalance is $\text{SMD} = 0.035$ (Section~11 statutory
basis), well within the acceptable range. The
effective sample size of the reweighted pre-PSLRA cohort
is 578 (56.0\% of the unweighted $n = 1{,}031$),
indicating that moderate information loss accompanies
the weighting but that the composition-adjusted estimates
retain meaningful statistical power---as confirmed by
all eight IPTW hazard ratios achieving $p < 0.001$
(Table~\ref{tab:iptw_triangulation}).

Figure~\ref{fig:ps_overlap} illustrates the positivity challenge underlying
this analysis. The propensity score distributions for pre- and post-PSLRA
cases overlap only in the upper range of the propensity-score
distribution, concentrated roughly between 0.75 and 0.95,
reflecting the 11.5:1 post-to-pre imbalance inherent to the
legislative date cutoff. Cases in regions of near-zero
overlap receive extreme ATT weights; trimming at the 99th
percentile (cap $\approx 43.5$) discards the most extreme
pre-PSLRA reweightings and reduces the effective sample size
to 578.

Two caveats apply. First, balance on observed covariates
does not guarantee balance on unobserved confounders. The
IPTW estimates adjust for observable compositional shifts
in the case mix but cannot address unmeasured changes in
judicial attitudes, plaintiff-bar sophistication, or
economic conditions across the 34-year study window.
Second, several covariates---circuit filing location,
MDL consolidation, case origin---may themselves be
consequences of the PSLRA rather than pre-treatment
confounders. If the PSLRA altered where and how cases were
filed, then adjusting for these covariates conditions on
potential downstream channels of the reform. The
composition-adjusted HR should therefore be interpreted as
the portion of the association that remains after
conditioning on those observables, not as a literal lower
bound on the total PSLRA association.

\subsection{Frailty Variance}
\label{subsec:frailty_diagnostics}

The shared frailty models in
Section~\ref{sec:results_circuits} introduce a
circuit-level random intercept to capture unobserved
heterogeneity across judicial circuits. The key diagnostic
quantity is the frailty variance $\hat{\theta}$, which
measures the magnitude of unobserved between-circuit
variation on the log-hazard scale.

For the extended settlement model, $\hat{\theta} = 0.130$,
suggesting modest residual circuit-level heterogeneity
beyond what the fixed-effect covariates capture. For the
extended dismissal model, $\hat{\theta} = 0.040$,
indicating that the covariate-adjusted dismissal hazard
is comparatively uniform across circuits. The contrast
is consistent with settlement outcomes being somewhat
more sensitive to local judicial culture and bar practices
than dismissal outcomes, which are more tightly governed
by federal procedural standards.

The small-cluster limitation discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:frailty} applies; the frailty analysis is
a sensitivity check whose primary value lies in confirming
that the PSLRA hazard ratio is virtually unchanged between
fixed-effect and random-effect specifications
($\Delta \text{HR} < 0.002$ for both outcomes).

\section{Summary of Findings}
\label{sec:results_summary}

This section closes the empirical chapter with two broad
conclusions. The dismissal association is the stable
finding: it is positive in every specification,
concentrated early in case life, and still above unity after
composition adjustment. The settlement association is
materially weaker, attenuating under time-trend control and
failing the cluster-robust and major circuit-specific
checks. Competing-risks methods also expose heterogeneity
that single-outcome models would miss, especially circuit
geography and the MDL sign reversal. Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion}
interprets these patterns and the identification limits that
bound them.
