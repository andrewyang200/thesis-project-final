This chapter presents the empirical results of the
competing-risks survival analysis described in
Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology}. We proceed from nonparametric
characterization of duration and outcomes
(Section~\ref{sec:results_overview}), through layered
assessment of the PSLRA's association with case resolution
(Section~\ref{sec:results_pslra}), geographic disparity
across federal circuits
(Section~\ref{sec:results_circuits}), and case-level
structural characteristics
(Section~\ref{sec:results_characteristics}), to model
diagnostics (Section~\ref{sec:results_diagnostics}) and a
summary of findings (Section~\ref{sec:results_summary}).
The organizing question throughout is: how do the PSLRA,
circuit geography, and case characteristics jointly shape
the timing and type of resolution in federal securities
class actions?

\section{Overview: Duration and Competing Outcomes}
\label{sec:results_overview}

\subsection{Overall Duration}

Figure~\ref{fig:km_overall} presents the Kaplan-Meier
estimate of the overall survival function for the full
analysis sample of 12,968 federal securities class actions.
The curve drops steeply in the first two years, reflecting
the concentration of dismissals during the
motion-to-dismiss phase: approximately 33\% of cases are
resolved within the first year, and roughly 52\% within two
years. Resolution slows markedly after year four, with fewer
than 5\% of cases remaining pending beyond eight years.

\subsection{Competing-Risks Cumulative Incidence: Overall}

Figure~\ref{fig:cif_overall} decomposes overall resolution
into its two competing terminal events. Dismissal incidence
climbs rapidly: by two years post-filing, approximately 44\%
of cases have been dismissed, and by five years the cumulative
dismissal probability approaches 67\%. Settlement incidence
accumulates more slowly, reaching approximately 24\% by
eight years. The two curves do not converge, reflecting the
fundamental asymmetry between the two outcomes: dismissal
is the dominant resolution pathway, operating primarily
through early pleading-stage motions, while settlement is
a comparatively rare and temporally dispersed event
concentrated among cases that survive the initial
motion-to-dismiss.

A naive Kaplan-Meier estimate of time to settlement that
censors dismissals would produce upwardly biased settlement
probabilities by treating dismissed cases as though they
remained at risk of eventual settlement---precisely the
type of bias the Aalen-Johansen competing-risks estimator
avoids. The dominance of dismissal as a resolution pathway
and its temporal concentration in the first two years
provide the empirical baseline against which we now assess
whether the PSLRA systematically altered these patterns.

\section{The PSLRA and Litigation Outcomes}
\label{sec:results_pslra}

The PSLRA is associated with a substantial increase in dismissal hazard
and a corresponding suppression of settlement probability
across the entire 1990--2024 observation window. This section
builds the evidence in five layers---nonparametric patterns,
associational estimates, time-varying dynamics,
composition-adjusted inference, and robustness
checks---each progressively narrowing the range of plausible
explanations for the observed divergence.

\subsection{Nonparametric Evidence: CIF by PSLRA Regime}

Figure~\ref{fig:cif_pslra} stratifies the cumulative
incidence functions by PSLRA regime. Post-PSLRA dismissal
incidence rises sharply in the first year and reaches 68.1\%
by five years, substantially above the pre-PSLRA trajectory
of 49.2\%. Post-PSLRA settlement incidence is simultaneously
suppressed, reaching only 17.0\% at five years compared
with 41.8\% pre-PSLRA. The reform is associated with
divergent shifts in both competing processes simultaneously
and in opposite directions.

\begin{figure}[htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig_cif_pslra.png}
    \vskip 5pt
    \caption[Cumulative Incidence of Outcomes by PSLRA Regime]{Aalen-Johansen CIFs stratified by PSLRA regime. Solid lines = Pre-PSLRA ($n = 1{,}032$); dashed = Post-PSLRA ($n = 11{,}936$). Green = settlement; orange/red = dismissal. Gray's test rejects equality for both settlement (statistic $= 302$, $p < 0.001$) and dismissal (statistic $= 167$, $p < 0.001$).}
    \label{fig:cif_pslra}
\end{figure}

Table~\ref{tab:cif_horizons} quantifies differences at four
key horizons. The divergence is particularly pronounced in the
first year: one-year dismissal incidence is 30.9\%
post-PSLRA versus 17.6\% pre-PSLRA, consistent with the
PSLRA's heightened pleading requirements concentrating
dismissals at the pleading stage. The settlement gap widens
steadily: the five-year settlement difference is 24.8
percentage points (41.8\% vs.\ 17.0\%), indicating a sustained
divergence in outcome probabilities rather than merely a
timing shift. Notably, pre-PSLRA five-year dismissal and
settlement CIFs are within 7.4 percentage points of each
other (49.2\% vs.\ 41.8\%), suggesting that under the old
regime the two outcomes were roughly equiprobable for
long-duration cases. Post-PSLRA, dismissal probability
exceeds settlement probability by a factor of four or more
at every horizon.

\subsection{Associational Evidence: Baseline Cause-Specific Cox Models}

Table~\ref{tab:cox_baseline} presents baseline cause-specific
Cox models with the PSLRA indicator as the sole covariate.
Post-PSLRA filing is associated with a 55\% lower
instantaneous settlement hazard (hazard ratio
[HR] $= 0.445$, 95\% CI: 0.401--0.495, $p < 0.001$) and a
47\% higher instantaneous dismissal hazard (HR $= 1.468$,
95\% CI: 1.342--1.606, $p < 0.001$). In a competing-risks setting, a hazard ratio above one indicates a higher instantaneous rate of experiencing that specific outcome (faster time-to-that-outcome); a hazard ratio below one indicates a lower instantaneous rate. Because settlement and dismissal are competing events, a higher cause-specific hazard for one outcome does not guarantee shorter overall case duration---it reflects the instantaneous rate of that particular terminal event, holding the competing pathway fixed. However, as detailed in
Section~\ref{sec:results_robustness}, the settlement
association is highly sensitive to time-trend controls and
loses statistical significance entirely in circuit-specific
sub-samples.

The Grambsch-Therneau test rejects the proportional hazards

hazard rates is not constant over case duration. This
motivates the time-varying analysis in
Section~\ref{sec:piecewise} and implies that the constant
hazard ratios above are best interpreted as time-averaged
summaries of a more complex temporal relationship.

\subsection{Time-Varying PSLRA Association with Dismissal Hazard}
\label{sec:piecewise}

Table~\ref{tab:piecewise} presents the piecewise constant
hazard model for dismissal. The temporal decomposition
reveals a distinctive three-phase pattern. In the first year,
post-PSLRA cases are associated with a 95\% higher
instantaneous dismissal hazard (HR $= 1.948$, 95\% CI:
1.675--2.266, $p < 0.001$): the PSLRA's heightened
pleading requirements and automatic discovery stay
concentrate dismissals at the pleading stage. In years one
to two, the PSLRA association vanishes entirely
(HR $= 1.050$, 95\% CI: 0.883--1.249, $p = 0.582$):
cases that survived the first-year gauntlet face no
differential dismissal risk. For cases surviving beyond two
years, a modest but significant PSLRA association
re-emerges (HR $= 1.329$, 95\% CI: 1.148--1.538,
$p < 0.001$).

This three-phase pattern---strong early association, null
middle-period, modest late re-emergence---is, to our
knowledge, the first formal documentation of the PSLRA's
time-varying signature using a piecewise survival framework
on population-level administrative data. The cutpoints at
$t = 1$ and $t = 2$ years were chosen heuristically to align
with the typical procedural timeline of a motion to dismiss
(see Section~\ref{sec:ph_diag} for the selection rationale).
The piecewise specification is applied to both outcomes for
symmetry; the dismissal model's global PH test does not
reject proportionality for the PSLRA indicator
($p = 0.120$), so the dismissal decomposition supplements
the settlement violation rather than correcting one of its
own.

\subsection{Composition-Adjusted Evidence: IPTW Triangulation}
\label{sec:results_iptw}

The preceding analyses establish that the PSLRA is associated
with large and statistically significant differences in
hazard rates. A natural question is how much of this raw
association reflects the reform itself, and how much reflects
concurrent changes in observable case composition. If the
post-PSLRA period sees disproportionately more cases filed
in high-dismissal circuits, for instance, part of the raw
PSLRA association could be an artifact of compositional
shifts rather than a consequence of the reform.

To decompose the raw PSLRA association, we apply the
four-strategy triangulation framework described in
Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology}, using inverse probability of
treatment weighting to re-weight the pre-PSLRA
control sample to match the post-PSLRA case mix on observed
covariates. The IPTW analysis uses 12,866 cases with
complete covariate data (102 cases dropped due to missing
origin or statutory basis); the slight difference in
unadjusted HRs relative to Table~\ref{tab:cox_baseline}
reflects this restricted sample.
Table~\ref{tab:iptw_triangulation} presents the results,
progressing from no adjustment to full IPTW-weighted
estimation.

All eight estimates (four strategies $\times$ two outcomes)

comparison is between the unadjusted estimate (row~1) and the

For dismissal, the composition-adjusted HR is 1.526,
\emph{slightly amplified} from the unadjusted 1.466. After
adjusting for observable shifts in circuit composition, case
origin, and statutory basis, the PSLRA remains associated
with a 53\% higher instantaneous dismissal hazard. The
amplification indicates that concurrent compositional changes
in the case mix---such as shifts in circuit filing
patterns---partially \emph{offset} the PSLRA's
dismissal-accelerating association; removing this confounding
reveals a somewhat larger underlying divergence.

For settlement, the picture is different. The
composition-adjusted HR is 0.690, substantially attenuated
from the unadjusted 0.439. The raw 56\% reduction in
settlement hazard shrinks to a 31\% reduction after
composition adjustment. Approximately half of the raw
settlement suppression is associated with observable shifts in the case mix across the pre- and post-PSLRA periods---shifts in circuit composition, statutory basis mix, and case origins. The residual association is not causally attributable to the reform; unmeasured temporal confounders may account for part or all of it.

An important caveat applies to the amplification comparison: Table~\ref{tab:iptw_triangulation} reports point estimates but not confidence intervals or standard errors for the MSM (row~4) and covariate-adjusted IPTW (row~3) strategies. The observed shift from 1.466 to 1.526 for dismissal is a point-estimate comparison that has not been formally tested; bootstrapped inference for these specifications is deferred to future work. The amplification narrative should therefore be interpreted as suggestive, not as a statistically confirmed divergence.

Five observations warrant emphasis. First, Schemes~B and~C
reclassify Code~12 (voluntary dismissal) and Code~5 (consent
judgment) as settlements, producing settlement HRs of
$\approx 0.72$ compared with 0.445 under Scheme~A. The
PSLRA settlement association is sensitive to disposition
coding but remains large and significant under all schemes.
Dismissal HRs attenuate modestly (1.321 vs.\ 1.468) under
liberal coding.

Second, excluding post-2020 cases has virtually no effect on
estimates, indicating that recently filed cases with high
censoring rates do not distort the results.

Third, the circuit-specific sub-models provide the most
important qualification of the settlement finding. Within
the Second Circuit, the PSLRA's association with settlement
hazard is non-significant (HR $= 0.980$, $p = 0.953$), and
within the Ninth Circuit the association does not reach
conventional significance (HR $= 0.869$, $p = 0.129$). The
PSLRA dismissal association, by contrast, is significant and
substantial in both circuits (Second: HR $= 1.748$; Ninth:
HR $= 1.804$). This pattern suggests that the pooled
settlement HR reflects between-circuit composition effects
as much as within-circuit reform associations.

Fourth, the spline time-trend control is the strongest
identification test: after flexibly absorbing nonlinear
secular trends in judicial behavior via a natural cubic
spline ($\text{df} = 3$), the PSLRA dismissal HR
\emph{increases} to 2.449 ($p < 0.001$), confirming that
the dismissal association is not a calendar-time artifact.
The settlement HR attenuates to 0.610 and remains significant
($p = 0.001$) under the spline specification, but the
attenuation from 0.445 indicates that part of the raw
settlement suppression is confounded with secular trends.

Fifth, across all seven specifications the dismissal HR
ranges from 1.32 to 2.45 and the settlement HR ranges from
0.44 to 0.98. The dismissal direction is unanimous and
statistically significant in every specification. The
settlement direction is consistent (all HRs $< 1$) but
loses significance in circuit sub-samples.

\subsubsection*{Temporal Identification and Placebo Results}

To further probe whether the observed PSLRA association
reflects the reform itself or pre-existing trends, we
implement the identification strategies described in
Section~\ref{sec:temporal_id}.

\textbf{Clean-window analysis (1993--1998).} Restricting the
sample to a narrow six-year window ($n = 1{,}235$)
surrounding the PSLRA's effective date yields a dismissal
HR of 1.394 (95\% CI: 1.190--1.633, $p < 0.001$) and a
settlement HR of 0.733 (95\% CI: 0.607--0.884, $p = 0.001$).
Both associations are attenuated relative to the full-sample
baseline, as expected in a restricted window with less
statistical power, but they remain significant and
directionally consistent.

\textbf{Placebo cutoff test (1992).} As a falsification
check, we construct a placebo ``law'' at January~1, 1992 and
re-estimate on the pre-PSLRA sample only ($n = 1{,}032$,
spanning 1990--1995). The placebo produces a significant
dismissal HR of 0.719 (95\% CI: 0.597--0.867, $p < 0.001$),
indicating that the model detects pre-existing trends in
dismissal rates during the early 1990s \emph{even in the
absence of any legislative change}. This result has two
implications. First, it reinforces the importance of flexible
time-trend controls: a simple before-after comparison may
partially reflect secular judicial drift. Second, it
strengthens our decision to frame all estimates as
associational or composition-adjusted rather than causally
identified. The PSLRA's association is robust and substantial
across specifications, but the pre-existing trend detected
by the placebo test precludes clean causal attribution to the
reform in isolation.

\subsubsection*{Synthesis: The Weight of Evidence}

The five evidence layers converge on a clear pattern.
The PSLRA is associated with higher dismissal hazard across
every model and specification, with a composition-adjusted
estimate of HR $\approx 1.53$ (MSM) and a full range of
1.32--2.45 across robustness checks. The dismissal
association is concentrated in the first year of case life
(piecewise HR $= 1.948$), vanishes in the second year, and
re-emerges modestly thereafter. This three-phase temporal
signature is robust to composition adjustment and spline
time-trend controls.

The PSLRA's association with settlement suppression is
directionally consistent across all specifications
(all HRs $< 1$) but more fragile. The raw settlement HR of
0.445 attenuates to 0.690 after composition adjustment
(Table~\ref{tab:iptw_triangulation}) and to 0.610 under
flexible time-trend controls
(Table~\ref{tab:robustness}). Critically, the settlement
association loses statistical significance entirely within
individual circuits (Second Circuit: HR $= 0.980$,
$p = 0.953$; Ninth Circuit: HR $= 0.869$, $p = 0.129$).
The 1992 placebo test, detecting significant pre-existing
trends before the reform was enacted, further limits causal
attribution. The settlement finding should be interpreted
as a robust population-level pattern whose attribution to
the PSLRA specifically, rather than to concurrent secular
trends, cannot be established with these data.

\section{Geographic Disparity Across Federal Circuits}
\label{sec:results_circuits}

Circuit geography is the dominant predictor of securities litigation outcomes. Settlement hazard ratios relative to the Second Circuit span a nearly 18-fold range across circuits, dwarfing the magnitude of the PSLRA association. Moreover, the PSLRA's association with outcomes is not uniform across jurisdictions: likelihood ratio tests reject a homogeneous PSLRA coefficient in favor of circuit-specific responses, and a shared frailty analysis confirms that substantial unobserved heterogeneity remains even after conditioning on observed covariates.

\subsection{Cumulative Incidence by Circuit}

Figures~\ref{fig:cif_circuit_dismissal}
and~\ref{fig:cif_circuit_settlement} present CIFs for the
four largest circuits by case volume plus the Sixth Circuit.
Circuit heterogeneity is substantial, and the patterns
are not symmetric across outcomes---a nuanced finding
that the competing-risks framework reveals and that
standard single-outcome analyses would miss.

For dismissal (Figure~\ref{fig:cif_circuit_dismissal}),
the Second Circuit exhibits the highest long-run dismissal
incidence, reaching approximately 75\% by six years. The
Fifth Circuit leads in early dismissal---nearly 40\% by year
one---reflecting aggressive early motion practice in Texas
and Louisiana. The Third Circuit has the lowest overall
dismissal incidence among these circuits, plateauing near 50\%.

The settlement picture is qualitatively different
(Figure~\ref{fig:cif_circuit_settlement}). The Third Circuit
has the highest settlement incidence, reaching approximately
30\% by year three. The Second Circuit has the lowest
settlement incidence of any major circuit, barely reaching
5\% by six years. This combination---low settlement
probability and high dismissal incidence---is the defining
empirical signature of the Second Circuit.

\subsection{Shared Frailty Sensitivity Analysis}

heterogeneity---differences in judicial culture, local bar
practices, or case-management norms not captured by the
covariates---further shapes outcomes. We fit shared frailty
models (Gaussian random intercepts on the log-hazard scale

that the 11 circuits fall below the 20--30 clusters typically
recommended for reliable variance estimation.

($\hat{\theta} = 0.033$), suggesting that the fixed-effect
circuit indicators already capture most circuit-level

\section{Model Diagnostics and Validation}
\label{sec:results_diagnostics}

This section evaluates the assumptions underlying the
models presented in
Sections~\ref{sec:results_pslra}--\ref{sec:results_characteristics}
and quantifies their predictive performance. We address
four diagnostic dimensions: proportional hazards testing,
covariate balance under IPTW weighting, frailty variance
estimation, and out-of-sample discrimination.

\subsection{IPTW Covariate Balance}
\label{subsec:iptw_balance}

The validity of the composition-adjusted estimates in
Section~\ref{sec:results_pslra} depends on the IPTW
weights achieving adequate balance between the pre-PSLRA
($n = 1{,}031$) and post-PSLRA ($n = 11{,}835$) cohorts on
all observed covariates. We use ATT (average treatment
effect on the treated) weights with 99th-percentile
trimming (cap $\approx 43.5$), which reweight the smaller
pre-PSLRA cohort to match the post-PSLRA covariate
distribution.

Figure~\ref{fig:iptw_balance} presents the Love plot of
standardized mean differences before and after
weighting. After weighting, all 19 covariate levels
achieve $|\text{SMD}| < 0.10$, the conventional threshold
for adequate balance \citep{austinfine2025}. The largest
residual imbalance is $\text{SMD} = 0.035$ (Section~11
statutory basis), well within the acceptable range. The
effective sample size of the reweighted pre-PSLRA cohort
is 578 (56.0\% of the unweighted $n = 1{,}031$),
indicating that moderate information loss accompanies
the weighting but that the composition-adjusted estimates
retain meaningful statistical power---as confirmed by
all eight IPTW hazard ratios achieving $p < 0.001$
(Table~\ref{tab:iptw_triangulation}).

Figure~\ref{fig:ps_overlap} illustrates the positivity challenge underlying
this analysis. The propensity score distributions for pre- and post-PSLRA
cases overlap only in a narrow central range, reflecting the 11.6:1
post-to-pre imbalance inherent to the legislative date cutoff. Cases in
regions of near-zero overlap receive extreme ATT weights; trimming at the
99th percentile (cap $\approx 43.5$) discards the most extreme pre-PSLRA
reweightings at the cost of 11 observations and reduces the ESS from
approximately 600 to 578.

Two caveats apply. First, balance on observed covariates
does not guarantee balance on unobserved confounders. The
IPTW estimates adjust for observable compositional shifts
in the case mix but cannot address unmeasured changes in
judicial attitudes, plaintiff-bar sophistication, or
economic conditions across the 34-year study window.
Second, several covariates---circuit filing location,
MDL consolidation, case origin---may themselves be
consequences of the PSLRA rather than pre-treatment
confounders. If the PSLRA altered where and how cases were
filed, then adjusting for these covariates removes part
of the PSLRA's effect pathway, and the composition-adjusted
HR is best interpreted as a lower bound on the total
PSLRA association (see Section~\ref{sec:results_pslra}).

\subsection{Frailty Variance}
\label{subsec:frailty_diagnostics}

The shared frailty models in
Section~\ref{sec:results_circuits} introduce a
circuit-level random intercept to capture unobserved
heterogeneity across judicial circuits. The key diagnostic
quantity is the frailty variance $\hat{\theta}$, which
measures the magnitude of unobserved between-circuit
variation on the log-hazard scale.

For the extended settlement model, $\hat{\theta} = 0.449$,
suggestive of unobserved circuit-level heterogeneity beyond what covariates explain, though the 11-cluster constraint makes this estimate highly imprecise. For the
extended dismissal model, $\hat{\theta} = 0.033$,
indicating that the covariate-adjusted dismissal hazard
is comparatively uniform across circuits. The contrast
is consistent with settlement outcomes being more
sensitive to local judicial culture and bar practices
than dismissal outcomes, which are more tightly governed
by federal procedural standards.

These estimates carry an important limitation: the frailty
models use only 11 circuit-level clusters (circuits with
$\geq 50$ cases), below the 20--30 clusters typically
recommended for reliable variance component estimation.
With so few clusters, the frailty variance may be biased
downward, and confidence intervals for $\hat{\theta}$ are
unreliable. For this reason, the frailty analysis is
framed as a sensitivity analysis throughout---its primary
value lies in confirming that the PSLRA hazard ratio is
virtually unchanged between fixed-effect and
random-effect specifications ($\Delta \text{HR} < 0.002$
for both outcomes) and in providing cluster-robust standard
errors that widen the confidence intervals appropriately
(Section~\ref{sec:results_circuits}).

\section{Summary of Findings}
\label{sec:results_summary}

This section consolidates the principal results of the
competing-risks analysis. We organize findings into two
tiers: associational results from the cause-specific Cox
and Fine-Gray models (Tier~1), and composition-adjusted
results from the IPTW analysis that account for observable
shifts in the case mix across the pre- and post-PSLRA
periods (Tier~2). Unless otherwise noted, all hazard ratios
are from the extended specification with full covariates.

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{The PSLRA is associated with a temporally structured elevation in dismissal hazard---concentrated in the first year, null in the second, and modestly re-emergent thereafter (Tier~1)---and this association survives composition adjustment (Tier~2).} The extended
cause-specific Cox model yields a dismissal hazard ratio
of $\text{HR} = 1.751$ ($p < 0.001$), and the
composition-adjusted IPTW marginal structural estimate is
$\text{HR} = 1.526$ ($p < 0.001$). Across all seven
robustness specifications---alternative disposition coding
schemes, temporal restrictions, circuit sub-samples, and
flexible spline time-trend controls---the dismissal HR
ranges from 1.32 to 2.45, with none crossing unity.

\item \textbf{The PSLRA's association with dismissal is
concentrated in the first year of litigation and exhibits a
three-phase temporal pattern (Tier~1).} The piecewise Cox
model shows a strong early-phase effect (0--1 year:
$\text{HR} = 1.948$, $p < 0.001$), no detectable
association during the second year ($\text{HR} = 1.050$,
$p = 0.582$), and a modest re-emergence beyond two years
($\text{HR} = 1.329$, $p < 0.001$). This temporal
structure aligns with the PSLRA's heightened pleading
standards, which primarily affect the motion-to-dismiss
stage.

\item \textbf{The PSLRA is associated with settlement
suppression, but this association is fragile under
alternative specifications (Tiers~1 and~2).} The
baseline cause-specific Cox model estimates a settlement
$\text{HR} = 0.445$ ($p < 0.001$), which attenuates to
0.690 ($p < 0.001$) after IPTW composition adjustment
and to 0.610 ($p = 0.001$) under flexible spline
time-trend controls. The settlement association loses
statistical significance entirely in circuit-specific
sub-samples (Second Circuit: $\text{HR} = 0.980$,
$p = 0.953$; Ninth Circuit: $\text{HR} = 0.869$,
$p = 0.129$). A 1992 placebo cutoff test detects
significant pre-existing trends in the pre-PSLRA period
(dismissal $\text{HR} = 0.719$, $p < 0.001$), further
limiting the attributability of the settlement-suppression
association---and all PSLRA associations---to the reform
itself.

\item \textbf{Circuit geography is the dominant structural
predictor of case outcomes, with substantial unobserved
heterogeneity in settlement but not dismissal (Tiers~1
and~2).} Circuit indicators account for the largest share
of explained variation in both Cox models. The shared
frailty model estimates a settlement frailty variance of
$\hat{\theta} = 0.449$---suggestive of unobserved
circuit-level heterogeneity beyond what covariates
capture, though highly uncertain with only 11 clusters---versus $\hat{\theta} = 0.033$ for dismissal.
This contrast suggests that settlement outcomes are
more sensitive to local judicial culture and bar
practices, while dismissal outcomes are more tightly
governed by federal procedural standards. The 11-cluster
limitation warrants caution in interpreting the magnitude
of $\hat{\theta}$, but the qualitative settlement--dismissal
contrast is robust.

\item \textbf{The PSLRA's associations are heterogeneous
across circuits (Tier~1).} Interaction models reveal that the
reform's settlement suppression is weakest in the Second
Circuit and its dismissal amplification is most strongly
attenuated in the Fourth and Eleventh Circuits. This geographic
heterogeneity implies that the PSLRA's practical impact
depends on where a case is filed.

\item \textbf{MDL consolidation suppresses both
cause-specific hazards, but competing-risks analysis
reveals a sign reversal invisible to single-outcome
models (Tier~1).} The cause-specific Cox model estimates
that MDL cases face substantially lower instantaneous
settlement and dismissal rates. However, the Fine-Gray
subdistribution model yields a modestly \emph{higher}
cumulative settlement probability
($\text{SHR} = 1.167$, $p = 0.217$): because MDL
cases are rarely dismissed, the pool available to
settle remains large throughout follow-up, and
cumulative settlement probability is not detectably
lower than in non-MDL cases despite the reduced
instantaneous rate.

\item \textbf{Observable covariates achieve moderate
predictive discrimination for settlement but weak
discrimination for dismissal.} The cause-specific Cox
model attains a C-index of 0.727 for settlement and
0.597 for dismissal on the held-out test set. The gap
indicates that dismissal outcomes depend more heavily on
unobserved case-merit factors---such as the strength of
the underlying legal claims, quality of counsel, and
evidentiary posture---than on the structural covariates
available in the IDB. This finding underscores both the
value and the limits of administrative data for modeling
litigation outcomes.

\end{enumerate}
